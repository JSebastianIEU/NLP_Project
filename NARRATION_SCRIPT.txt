───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[00:01]  SCENE 1 — TITLE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This video explains our NLP project: Semantic Similarity, Engagement Behavior,
and Explainable Hashtag Recommendation on TikTok — completed as part of the
Natural Language Processing course at IE University.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[00:15]  SCENE 2 — RESEARCH QUESTION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The central question: do TikTok posts with semantically similar captions form
neighborhoods with more homogeneous engagement profiles? We are not asking how
much engagement a post will receive — but whether meaning predicts consistency.

[00:27]
Our null hypothesis says proximity carries no engagement signal.
Our alternative says similar content performs more similarly than chance
predicts. H-one holds — uniformly — across all twelve statistical tests,
with p approximately zero.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[00:41]  SCENE 3 — DATA OVERVIEW
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
We loaded twenty-six thousand six hundred twenty-three TikTok posts from a
PostgreSQL database hosted on Supabase, through a normalised schema of four
tables: videos, video snapshots, video hashtags, and hashtags. Fields include
caption, likes, comments, views, shares, and timestamps.

[00:57]
After dropping captions with fewer than four non-hashtag characters, we kept
twenty-two thousand six hundred forty-seven posts. A critical discovery: the
hashtag bridge table covered only three-point-nine percent of posts. Our
two-phase extraction — combining inline caption hashtags with the bridge table
— raised coverage to one hundred percent, averaging five-point-five-eight
hashtags per post. This fix is not a preprocessing nicety. It is a prerequisite
for the entire recommendation task.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[01:23]  SCENE 4 — PREPROCESSING PIPELINE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cleaning follows a strict order. First, we extract hashtags — the label. Then
we remove them from the text, so the model learns content, not tags. Emojis are
preserved for SBERT, which handles Unicode natively. For TF-IDF, emojis are
demojized into text tokens — so a flexed biceps becomes the word
"flexed_biceps", a countable vocabulary item. Finally, log-one-plus-y
transformation compresses the heavy-tailed engagement distributions.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[01:56]  SCENE 5 — SBERT EMBEDDINGS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Each cleaned caption is encoded by Sentence-BERT — specifically the
all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model — producing a three-hundred-eighty-four dimensional
embedding vector. After L2 normalisation, cosine similarity equals the dot
product directly: the closer two vectors, the more similar their meaning.
Removing hashtags before encoding is critical — without this step, SBERT would
embed hashtag-string similarity rather than topical content.

[02:18]
UMAP projects the three-eighty-four dimensional embeddings into two dimensions
for visualisation. The resulting scatter plot reveals clear topical clusters —
fitness, gaming, food, crypto — demonstrating that the embedding space captures
genuine semantic organisation, not surface-level keyword overlap.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[02:43]  SCENE 6 — SEMANTIC NEIGHBORHOODS & STATISTICS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For each post, we build a K-nearest-neighbor index using FAISS — an exact
cosine search over L2-normalised vectors. For K in five, ten, and twenty, we
retrieve the most semantically similar posts. The query post itself is excluded
from its own neighborhood.

[03:01]
The key statistical question: is the within-group variance of log-engagement
metrics in semantic neighborhoods significantly lower than in random groups of
equal size K? We compute within-group variance for every neighborhood, then
compare against one thousand random baselines per K value using the Mann-Whitney
U test with alternative equals less — testing whether semantic variances are
stochastically smaller than random variances.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[03:29]  SCENE 7 — CLASSIFICATION BASELINES
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Before the structural test, we establish classification baselines. Four models
— Logistic Regression on SBERT embeddings, Multinomial Naive Bayes on TF-IDF,
and KNN classifiers at k-five and k-ten — all converge at approximately sixty
percent accuracy on binary engagement prediction. This convergence is the
information ceiling: caption text simply does not encode follower count, audio
choice, thumbnail quality, or algorithmic amplification — the true drivers of
TikTok engagement.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[04:03]  SCENE 8 — STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS RESULTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The structural analysis compares within-group variance across all twenty-two
thousand neighborhoods versus one thousand random-baseline simulations. All
twelve Mann-Whitney U tests — four engagement metrics times three K values —
returned p approximately zero. Variance reduction ranges from ten-point-five
percent to thirteen percent.

[04:19]
The heatmap shows variance reduction percentages across all conditions. At K
equals five, likes show a thirteen-point-zero-four percent reduction. The line
plot reveals the geometric signature: a consistent monotonic decay from K-five
to K-twenty across all four metrics. Tighter neighborhoods have stronger
semantic coherence. As the boundary expands, variance drifts back toward the
corpus-wide random baseline. This is precisely what genuine semantic-engagement
coupling should produce.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[04:47]  SCENE 9 — HASHTAG RECOMMENDER
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The structural finding enables a principled explainable hashtag recommender.
Given a new caption, we encode it with SBERT, retrieve its K nearest neighbors
in the corpus, and aggregate their hashtags — weighted by frequency and average
engagement. For the input "Just hit a new personal record at the gym — hard
work always pays off," the system returns tags like gymtok, fitness-for-all, and
workout-motivation. Every recommendation traces back to real semantically
similar posts in the corpus — it is fully explainable by design.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[05:15]  SCENE 10 — CONCLUSION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The conclusion: semantically similar captions do not predict engagement level —
the sixty percent accuracy ceiling confirms that. But they do predict engagement
consistency — a ten to thirteen percent variance reduction, confirmed at p
approximately zero across all twelve tests. The embedding space carries a real
structural signal, and the recommender is grounded in it. It does not guess.
It learns from posts that actually mean what yours means.
